## Executive Summary

This is a niche academic-to-academic service with strong unit economics. The goal is not hockey-stick growth — it's a steady pipeline of 5–10 projects/month at $150–$750+, yielding $2,000–$5,000/month in side-hustle revenue. The strategy exploits David's existing academic network, institutional credibility, and the Cambridge University Press differentiator to build a referral-driven flywheel with near-zero customer acquisition cost.

**The single biggest gap is discoverability.** The service is well-positioned, well-priced, and well-differentiated — but without active marketing, the target audience may never find it.

## 2. AI Impact Assessment

- **Short-term (2026–2027): LOW THREAT.** AI can't reliably produce submission-ready LaTeX for complex documents. Academics who try and fail become *better* leads.
- **Medium-term (2028–2030): MODERATE.** Simple documents (CVs, short papers) may become AI-handleable. Erodes the $150 tier but not $300–$750+.
- **Opportunity:** Position an "AI Rescue" service for academics who tried ChatGPT, got broken LaTeX, and need someone to fix it. Content marketing on "What ChatGPT gets wrong about LaTeX" targets high-intent search traffic.

## 4. Platform Strategy

### Tier 1 — Active Presence (Primary channels)

**Twitter/X (@dadamsAU)** — Densest concentration of target audience. LaTeX frustration tweets = organic lead generation.

**LinkedIn** — Underused by academics but growing for professional services. Cambridge UP credential carries enormous weight. Algorithm favors document carousels (before/after PDFs).

**Reddit** — r/LaTeX (190K+), r/GradSchool (500K+), r/AskAcademia (300K+). Answer questions. Be helpful. Let your profile link do the selling.

- **Bluesky** — Claim handle, cross-post from Twitter
- **Mastodon (scholar.social)** — Skews STEM/already-knows-LaTeX; partial audience mismatch
- **Facebook Groups** — Algorithm buries links; occasional participation only

### What NOT to Build

No YouTube channel. No podcast. No Instagram. No email newsletter yet. At 3–4 hours/week, concentrate effort on Twitter + LinkedIn + Reddit.

## 8. Growth Hacking Tactics

### The "Acknowledgments Section" Hack

Add to every deliverable `.tex` file:
```
% Typeset by David Adams — latex.dadams.io
% Questions about this template? latex@dadams.io
```
Every co-author, reviewer, or editor who opens the source sees it. Costs nothing. Compounds over every project.

### Structured Referral Program

"Refer a colleague who completes a project → your next revision round is free ($75 value)." Add "How did you hear about me?" to the contact form for attribution tracking.

### Department-Level Bundling

"3+ projects from the same department get 15% off each." One satisfied faculty member becomes a channel for their entire department.

### Before/After Share Mechanic

With client permission, create anonymized Word → LaTeX comparisons. These are inherently viral on Academic Twitter because they're visually dramatic.

### University Writing Center Outreach

Email graduate schools and writing centers at Cal State Fullerton and 5–10 regional universities. Offer to be listed as a recommended formatting resource. Many maintain referral lists for dissertation students.

### Overleaf Presence

1. **Apply for the Overleaf LaTeX Experts directory** — Free, high-visibility lead channel
2. **Submit 2–3 polished templates to the Overleaf Gallery** — Passive lead generation for years
